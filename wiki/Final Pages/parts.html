Rationales for Strains of Choice (WHO)



  •  It must be bacterial or caused by a DNA virus
  • It must be a common diseases (at least in one region)
  • It must be airborne (also contained in saliva) and contagious

  1. Tuberculosis

  1. one of the top 10 causes of global death (1.6million people died from TB in 2017, 230,000 children)
  2. Global incidence falling at 2% annually, needs to accelerate to 4-5% decline to reach 2020 milestone.
  3. 54 million lives saved through  TB diagnosis  and treatment 2000-2017
  4. Ending TB  epidemic by 2030 among health targets of Sustainable Development Goals
  5. Current diagnostic tool Xpert MTB/RIF essay generally takes 2 hours, hard to diagnose drug-resistant strains

  1. Hepatitis B

  1. Viral  infection that attacks the liver, leads to chronic infection and high risk of cirrhosis and liver cancer
  2. 257 million lives with chronic Hep B as of 2015
  3. Virus could survive outside of the body for more than 7 days and cause infection during this time
  4. Diagnosis usually made through blood tests

  1. Whooping Cough

  1. respiratory tract disease caused by pertussis bacteria most dangerous in infants
  2. spreads easily from person to person by coughing and sneezing
  3. around 144,000 reported cases in 2017, 89,000 estimated deaths in 2008
  4. Insufficient evidence for vaccine effectiveness in infants

  1. Plague

  1. Caused by bacteria found in fleas and small mammals, symptoms develop after 1-7 day incubation
  2. Infects animals and humans alike, transmitted by flea bites,  "direct contact with infected tissues, and inhalation  of respiratory droplets"
  3. high case-fatality ratio ("30%-60% for bubonic type, always fatal for pneumonic type when left untreated")
  4. 3,248 cases globally 2010-2015, 584 deaths
  5. "Early diagnosis and early treatment can save lives" since antibiotic treatment is effective
